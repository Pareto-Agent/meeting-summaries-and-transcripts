Stand-up 10.17 Morning
Team presented research on each stage of the renewals pipeline agent. Juliet provided feedback across all four components. Goal: get to a simple, consistent first prototype before adding complexity.
Qualification Layer (Aaron)
  • Normalize customer data into a standard schema (ARR, seat count, contract length, product) before running any models
  • Run two continuous checks: upsell signal (usage >125% for 2+ weeks, 120+ days from renewal) and churn risk (usage drop + news scan)
  • Output: a brief card for the rep — recommendation, usage trajectory, ARR impact, and a simple schema passed to the next agent
  • Juliet’s feedback:
    • Expand beyond usage signals — open support tickets, external company news, CRM call history
    • Tap LLM to read conversation history and surface direct quotes; goal is to meet or exceed the judgment of an average sales rep
    • Churn model should be flexible — use customer’s own model or offer an out-of-the-box version
    • Median price as a benchmark may be weak; compare to list price or deal pricing instead
    • 14-day usage drop window flagged as likely too short
  • Agreed: “high sell potential” should be passed as a binary flag, not a hard price recommendation — pricing logic lives downstream with Dean
Outreach Agent (Parth)
  • Validate champion, build email profile from communication history, draft outreach in the customer’s voice
  • Five sub-agents: happy path, red flags, at-risk, gone silent, champion departed
  • Language profiling: ingest prior emails to build a style guide, use as a second input alongside the draft prompt
  • Evan’s note: start from the customer’s existing email templates and iterate — LLMs tend to be wordy, so reverse-engineering tone from scratch is harder than building on top of real examples
  • Juliet’s feedback: liked the branching path logic and language profiling; wants to dig into the detail further
Pricing Proposal (Dean) + Buyer Portal (Cooper)
  • Dean: agent ingests signals from Aaron and Parth, decomposes existing PowerPoint templates into story components, reassembles into a tailored renewal deck
    • Pricing logic for v1: identify renewal increase vs. downsell risk — keep it simple
    • Key principle: the value story and the numbers must be consistent end-to-end
    • Juliet: value story is critical — include new features/capabilities released since last renewal; think through what “elements of a value story” look like structurally
    • Objection handling is out of scope for this agent — that goes to a separate agent downstream
  • Cooper: buyer-facing renewals hub — contract history, renewal options, quick-renew button, chatbot, ROI calculator, year-over-year comparison, per-unit pricing
    • ROI calculator should be configurable (buyer can change inputs); pricing stays fixed — no self-serve pricing exploration
    • Juliet: hub and PowerPoint should feel connected — hub is the workspace where buyers stress-test the story; PowerPoint is the shareable artifact for internal stakeholders
Strategic Principles
  • Keep v1 simple: get client buy-in on a simple model before adding edge cases — complexity introduced too early creates too many failure points
  • Variance is the biggest enemy of multi-step automation: a 7-step process with 5% variance per step compounds into an unworkable output
  • Work within seller systems first — don’t try to change how renewals work; make it easy for them to do things their way, and they may evolve toward better practices on their own
  • Evan referenced a call with Rol at Guard Square: radical pricing/usage transparency → high NRR, minimal negotiation overhead, small renewals team — worth keeping in mind as a long-term direction
